The Bramcote Old Church Tower's website re-design - behind the scene

The context -

This work came about as a friend told me about her involvement in the 13th-century Bramcote Old Church Tower and the surrounding de-consecrated graveyard restoration and maintenance. As a former art professional, I was intrigued by the project and went on the website to gather further information. After a first browse, I noticed that the church's history, a substantial detail, was missing altogether. The lack of this information made me wonder whether it was an intentional decision and what was the website's architectural information. Which needs were they trying to answer? After liaising with the charity's web designer and understanding the criteria used to structure the website, I suggested sending out an online survey and running a series of usability tests. The idea was to learn how people felt about the product now, its strength and pain points and how to implement those areas needing love.

The process -

In the fictional scenario, the user was interested in visiting the site and wanted to learn about the history and the ongoing project beforehand. They also wanted to know about the events and how to get involved in charity work. The first finding, the first headache - The usability test helped me understand where they expected to find the History section based on the mental model they've built through their experiences navigating websites with similar content. Participants looked for the History link in the global navigation bar next to the link The Project or as the link containing The Project. The History and The Project are related but to what extent? Would it be better to group History and Archaeology while separately showing The Project with its subcategories or creating a bespoke page for History and maintaining the rest as it was? The answer to this was to create various designs and test them again. The second finding was a little bit less of a headache - While reading the book Don't make me think by Steve Krug, I stumbled upon a question in which he invited the user to tell what struck them about the page, what they thought the website was about, how to navigate the page, and what kind of information were they receiving. Apart from comments on the aesthetic, it quickly appeared that the page needed to display a fundamental piece of information: directions and opening times. Their reaction made perfect sense! Imagine looking at a heritage site's website, falling in love with the pictures, getting excited to go for a trip, and then losing all your goodwill to find out how and when to get there. Struggle no more! To solve this problem, I used the competitive benchmarking research method to learn how the best in the world go about it. That showed me how certain information is repeated on multiple pages to improve the user flow and avoid making the person go back and forth from page to page.

Last but not least -

As mentioned, the charity that runs the Project is all volunteers based and apart from the events and memberships, donations are the other source of revenue. The BIG sensitive question - How can we prompt people to donate without being too pushy? The usability testing and asking people to talk about their previous experiences donating to a similar charity helped identify the triggers that could lead to a donation. While there are no fixed rules when supporting a cause, most users said that if they know where their contribution is going, they are happy to contribute. The solution, let's show them what we do!

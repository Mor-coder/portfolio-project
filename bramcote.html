The Bramcote Old Church Tower's website re-design - behind the scene

Act 1: The context

What? Why? How? -

Archaeological sites are like a portal to the past. They allow you to have a blurred sneak peek into the history of humanity. Nevertheless, despite their role as history representatives, they don't always receive the care and attention they deserve.

The de-consecrated 13th-century Bramcote Old Church Tower and the surrounding graveyard are one of those sites that, thanks to the dedication and passion of the local community still have a story to tell.

The desire to translate in the website the enthusiasm and hard work that the Friends of the Old Church Tower Trust puts daily into this project is what prompted me to offer my help to redesign the website.

Act 2: The research

Dig deeper, build empathy -

Comes first, I sat down with the web designer to discuss the what's, the whys and the hows behind the website's structure. Slowly but surely, more information on the website and the project's past, present, and future came to the surface, allowing me to grasp the Trust's mission and identity. We opted for runnig a series of Usability tests to identify how people felt about the website and to unfold the following:

  • Do people know who we are and what we do?
  • We need to add a new page called "History". Where should it go?
  • Do people know how to reach us?
  • Do they know the Trust run events and how to participate?

as well as more sensitive questions, like:

  • How do we motivate people to get involved?
  • How can we prompt them to donate without being too pushy?

The findings -

The Usability tests produced significant and insightful data that start shaping the face of the soon-to-be refreshed website. These included:

・Homepage - Participants' immediate needs included finding the directions to the site and useful information such as, opening hours, parking availability, and contacts.
・New page - When asked to look for the 'History' page (although still not existing) participants naturally look at the global navbar expecting to find the link there. Understanding their mental model was a significant cue for our re-design.
・Events page - The events page also need further details on how to participate.
・Donation & Getting involved - The tests showed that people are willing to get involved and donate if it is clear how the funds will be used and what for.

Competitive benchmarking, on the other hand, showed me that there seems to be conventional guidelines to websites' structure for cultural institutions. This finding was gold as it provided clear guidelines most likely matching peoples' mental model resulting in a smoother experience while navigating the site.

The challenges -

・Design a product that could be reproduced on Wordpress, the platform used by the Trust, and that it wasn't too complicated to implement.
・Designing a Homepage that offers a comprehensive idea of the Trust and the activities undertaken to preserve the archaelogical site. ・Incorporate elements such as directions and useful information on the Homepage and Events page without looking repetitive.
・Design the new 'History' page and find it a home.
・Prompt public's donations/participation.
